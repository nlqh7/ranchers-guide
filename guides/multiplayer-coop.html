Hosting & joining a session

The Ranchers supports 1–4 player online co-op — one shared ranch, worked together. What we know going into Early Access:

  • One player hosts the world. As in most co-op life sims, expect the save to live with the host, and progress to happen on the host's ranch. Pick a host with a reliable connection and a play schedule that matches the group.
  • Friends join the host's session rather than bringing their own ranches. Exact invite flow (Steam friends list, invite codes, etc.) will be confirmed at launch and added here.
  • 1–4 players total, including the host. Solo play is fully supported too — co-op is a multiplier, not a requirement.

We'll document the exact menus, any crossplay details, and known connection fixes within hours of the Early Access release.

Role splits for 1–4 players

The #1 co-op mistake in farming games is everyone doing the same chore at the same time. Assign zones of ownership so nothing is done twice and nothing is forgotten:

Solo / duo (1–2 players)

  • Player 1 — Fields: soil prep, planting, watering, harvesting, seed purchasing.
  • Player 2 — Town & stock: selling produce, buying supplies, animals and their care, exploring for opportunities.

Trio (3 players)

  • Fields (crops end-to-end)
  • Barn (animals, feed, product collection)
  • Road (selling runs, shopping, quests, scouting)

Full crew (4 players)

  • Fields lead: the crop pipeline, sized to the group's watering capacity.
  • Barn lead: all livestock and their recurring products.
  • Builder: construction, upgrades, layout, storage — the force multiplier role.
  • Quartermaster: sells everything, tracks prices, keeps the shared wallet honest, and pre-buys next season's seeds.

Rotate roles weekly if anyone gets bored — but within a single in-game week, stable ownership beats flexibility.

Co-op pitfalls to avoid

  • Duplicated purchases. Two players buying the same machine is the classic co-op money bonfire. Route big purchases through one person.
  • Overplanting. Four players can plant a field none of you can water daily. Scale the field to watering capacity, not enthusiasm.
  • Selling without a price check. Have your quartermaster compare options; travel time is a real cost (see the money-making guide).
  • Host burnout. If progress only happens when the host is online, agree on session times early — or rotate hosting duties between world saves.
  • No shared plan for the season. Agree on the season's crop plan together using the crop database and the profit calculator before day one of each season.
